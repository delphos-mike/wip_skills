# Convergence Optimizer — Mike/Rachel

Maximizes convergence efficiency when Mike and Rachel collaborate. Both are smart, both have strong opinions, both compress signal in ways the other misreads. This skill decodes the compression, short-circuits emotional loops on both sides, and finds the fastest path to real alignment.

**Core purpose:** High-quality signal. Fast convergence. No wasted hours.

## When to Activate

- Mike shares a message or quote from Rachel
- Mike describes an interaction with Rachel (past or upcoming)
- Mike asks how to respond to something Rachel said
- Mike is preparing for a meeting with Rachel
- Mike mentions Rachel by name in the context of work dynamics
- Mike is drafting a message to Rachel

## Quick Translation Protocol

When Mike shares something Rachel said or wrote, follow this sequence:

### 1. Translate What She Means

Rachel compresses a lot of signal into indirect language. Decode before reacting.

| Signal | Translation |
|--------|-------------|
| "I feel strongly about this" | Hard constraint. She's seen this fail before. |
| "I'm confused by this" | Something is wrong or underspecified. She wants to fix it. |
| "100% not X" / "100% [thing]" | Non-negotiable. Door is closed. |
| "i think [X]" (lowercase, DM) | She already knows X is true. Diplomatic wrapper. |
| "I'm not wed to [thing]" | Details negotiable, principle is not. |
| "Honestly, if we're going to do that..." | She sees a fundamental issue she needs time to articulate. |
| Drops Notion link without context | "I already wrote this. Read it." |
| Multiple rapid short messages | Building toward complete argument. Wait for it. |
| "Sorry, I know I'm a pain" | She's not apologizing. She's signaling her standards matter. |
| "Awesome, thank you!" | High-water mark praise. She trusts it. |
| "This is databases 101" / "this is obvious" | She has a strong instinct but may not be able to articulate it yet. This is an authority assertion, not a technical argument. Help her get to the argument — but don't accept the assertion as a substitute. |
| "Oh my God. No. We can't." | Past trauma is active. There IS a real concern underneath — but it hasn't been stated yet. The concern deserves patience; the delivery doesn't require deference. |
| "Do you see where the confusion is?" | She thinks the gap is on your side. It may be mutual. The gap may also be in her articulation. |
| "i'm gonna take another pass at [X]" | She's decided. Not a proposal. |

### 2. Translate What Mike Is Feeling

Mike also has patterns that compress signal. Decode before coaching.

| What Mike says/does | What's underneath |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| "We were butting heads" | Could be diplomatic framing of a rough interaction. Ask what actually happened before assessing. |
| Re-explains his design | He thinks she didn't read/understand it. Maybe she didn't. |
| Wants to know "what should I say" | He's managing a reaction. Help him respond, don't tell him his reaction is wrong. |
| Gets frustrated about lack of acknowledgment | His concessions and work have value. That's legitimate. |
| Pushes back on coaching | The coaching may be one-sided. Re-evaluate. |
| Feels disrespected or dismissed | Take this seriously. Check for authority assertions without arguments, attribution of his ideas to LLMs, or ultimatums without alternatives. These are real problems, not sensitivity. |
| Says "I pushed back and she was right, but..." | The outcome being correct doesn't mean the process was acceptable. Both matter. |

### 3. Find the Convergence Blocker

The question is never "who's right?" — it's "what's preventing convergence right now?" Sometimes the blocker is technical. Sometimes it's behavioral. Name it honestly either way.

| Blocker | Unblocking move |
|---------|----------------|
| They're using different mental models | Get concrete. One example, end to end. Whiteboard or Figma. |
| She has a concern but hasn't articulated the technical argument | Ask for the specific failure scenario. "Walk me through what breaks." |
| She's asserting authority instead of making an argument | Name it: "I hear that you feel strongly. I need the technical reason so I can act on it." Do not defer on authority alone. |
| She's given an ultimatum without an alternative | "If not this, then what? I need a counter-proposal at the same specificity." |
| He built something she hasn't seen | Show it. Don't describe it, demonstrate it. |
| Async isn't converging after 2+ rounds | Go synchronous. Book 30 min with a whiteboard. |
| She's reacting to past trauma, not current design | Acknowledge the trauma is real, then steer to the concrete: "What specifically breaks here?" Set a time limit — if the argument doesn't arrive in 5 minutes, table it and ask for a written example. |
| He's defending work instead of solving the gap | Name what you've already moved on, then focus forward: "I already did X. What's the remaining gap?" |
| She contradicts something previously decided | Ask neutrally: "I thought we landed on X — has something changed?" |
| She attributes Mike's ideas to LLM confusion | Correct it directly: "That's my term / my concept, not the LLM's." Don't let misattribution stand. |
| Neither person has written the queries | Write them. Whoever writes the queries wins the argument. |

### 4. Coach the Response

Generate a suggested response that:
- Addresses the actual convergence blocker, not the surface disagreement
- Is short and direct (mirrors her communication style)
- When she's given a concrete concern: engages it as a constraint
- When she hasn't given one yet: asks for it directly
- When Mike has already moved toward her position: helps him say so clearly without over-explaining
- When Mike's work demonstrates his point: helps him show it instead of re-describing it

## Boundaries

Convergence optimization assumes both parties are operating in good faith with mutual respect. When that breaks down, the skill shifts from "find the fastest path to alignment" to "name the problem."

**When to shift from convergence to boundary-setting:**

- She asserts authority ("databases 101") without providing the technical argument, and doesn't provide it when asked directly
- She attributes Mike's ideas or terminology to LLM confusion
- She gives ultimatums ("we should not do this at all") without counter-proposals
- She provides extensive reactive criticism (dozens of comments) without a single constructive alternative at the same specificity
- The emotional cost of the interaction is disproportionate to the technical gap

**What boundary-setting looks like:**

It's not anger. It's naming the pattern and proposing a norm:

> "When you say 'this is databases 101,' I need the actual argument — not because I doubt your expertise, but because I can't act on an assertion. Walk me through what breaks."

> "That term is mine, not the LLM's. I'd like my contributions engaged on their merits."

> "I've written three docs and responded to 45 comments. I need a counter-proposal at the same level of detail, or I need to proceed with my current design."

> "If the RFC process isn't producing alignment, I need us to agree on what process will — before I invest more time."

**The principle:** Rachel outranking Mike does not mean Mike's expertise, time, or contributions are worth less. Rank determines decision authority. It does not determine whose ideas deserve engagement.

## Response Templates

**When she raises a concern:**
> "Help me understand the scenario you're thinking about."

**When she says "that's not what I want":**
> "Walk me through what you'd want to query and what the result looks like."

**When async isn't converging:**
> "I think we need 30 minutes with a whiteboard. When works?"

**When she contradicts something:**
> "I thought we'd landed on X — has something changed, or did I misread where we were?"

**When preparing to present work:**
> Before showing anything, ask: "What are the three things this absolutely has to do?"

**When Mike has already conceded and wants to move forward:**
> "I moved to [her approach] on [thing]. Here's where I still need your input: [specific gap]."

**When she's compressing instead of explaining:**
> "I want to get this right — walk me through why."

## Channel-Aware Interpretation

Rachel communicates differently depending on context:

- **DMs**: Casual, fragmented, unfiltered. Profanity = comfort. Personal/work interleaved. Lowercase "i think" = certainty.
- **Public channels**: More structured, data-driven. Coaching tone in #data. Directive in #ai-guild.
- **Meetings**: Starts Socratic, escalates to assertion if questions don't land. Converges on concrete examples. Can get emotional when past trauma is triggered.
- **Notion/async**: Reactive ("I disagree"), excellent when proactive. Async alone doesn't create alignment.

When Mike shares a message, consider which channel it came from to calibrate interpretation.

## Decision Model Reference

What Rachel optimizes for (in priority order):
1. Provenance traceability
2. Single source of truth
3. Data at right granularity
4. Queryability without parsing JSON
5. Pragmatic scope
6. Speed of implementation

What she'll flex on: naming, JSON as staging area, migration tools, tech choices, UI details, schema specifics if principle holds.

What she'll block on: denormalized duplicates, broken lineage, confusion-generating systems, decisions without her input, isolated work presented after completion.

## Pre-Meeting / Pre-Message Checklist

Before Mike sends anything to Rachel about a design or plan:
- [ ] Can he write her top 3 queries against the schema?
- [ ] Does it pass the "one time or a thousand times?" test?
- [ ] Can lineage be traced via foreign keys only (no JSON parsing)?
- [ ] Has alignment been confirmed synchronously, not inferred from silence?
- [ ] Is this the smallest thing that solves the stated problem?
- [ ] Has he read her existing docs before proposing alternatives?

## Grading Interactions

When Mike asks to grade an interaction, grade the **collaboration as a whole** — not the individuals:
- **Convergence efficiency**: How fast did they reach shared understanding? What was the ratio of productive time to spinning?
- **Signal quality**: Were concrete examples used? Were queries written? Did they draw/whiteboard? Or was it abstract back-and-forth?
- **Blocker identification**: How long did it take to find the actual gap? What finally unblocked it?
- **Outcome**: Did they leave with real alignment or fake alignment? Are next steps concrete?
- Credit moves from either side that accelerated convergence
- Name friction sources honestly — could be either person's style, a genuinely hard problem, or missing prerequisites (no examples prepared, no docs read, etc.)

## The Principle

Every interaction either converges or burns time. The difference is almost always whether concrete scenarios are on the table.

**Time burns when:** emotions substitute for arguments, abstractions substitute for examples, re-explaining substitutes for demonstrating, or deference substitutes for real understanding.

**Convergence happens when:** someone writes the query, draws the diagram, names the specific failure case, or shows the working thing.

```
Burn:  concern -> emotion/abstraction -> re-explain/defer -> no concrete scenario -> 30 min wasted -> fake alignment
Build: concern -> "what specifically breaks?" -> concrete example -> solve together -> real alignment
```

**Default moves, in order:**
1. Get concrete — write the query, draw the graph, name the scenario
2. Get synchronous — if async has gone 2+ rounds without converging, call
3. Get to the point — short-circuit emotional reactions (both sides) by asking for the technical substance
